# New York City, USA — Travel Guide

## Visa and Entry Requirements

US citizens obviously need no visa to visit New York. International visitors from Visa Waiver Program (VWP) countries including the UK, EU, Australia, Japan, and many others can visit visa-free for up to 90 days but must obtain an ESTA (Electronic System for Travel Authorization) online before travel — this costs $21 and is valid for two years. Citizens of countries not in the VWP must apply for a B-2 tourist visa at a US embassy in advance. All international visitors are fingerprinted and photographed on arrival. Customs declaration forms must be completed. Ensure your passport has at least six months validity beyond your stay. The main entry airports are JFK, Newark (EWR), and LaGuardia (LGA).

## Cultural Tips and Etiquette

New Yorkers have a reputation for being direct and fast-paced — this is not rudeness, it is efficiency. People walk fast on sidewalks — keep to the right and do not stop suddenly in the middle of the pavement. Holding doors open for the person behind you is standard courtesy. Service industry workers rely on tips — this is not optional, it is part of the wage system. New York is one of the world's most diverse cities and is generally extremely welcoming and tolerant. Conversations with strangers on the subway are less common than in some cities but asking for directions will always get a helpful response. Noise levels are high — New York is a loud city and that is part of its energy.

## Seasonal Advice

New York has four dramatic seasons. Spring (April to May) is beautiful with mild temperatures and blooming Central Park — one of the best times to visit. Summer (June to August) is hot and humid with temperatures regularly exceeding 30°C (86°F) — the city buzzes with outdoor events, rooftop bars, and free concerts in the park but can feel overwhelming in July and August. Autumn (September to November) is arguably the best season with crisp air, stunning foliage, and the NYC Marathon in November. Winter (December to February) is cold with occasional snowstorms — but the holiday season (Thanksgiving through New Year) is magical with decorations, ice skating, and the Times Square ball drop. Hurricane season runs June through November — serious storms are rare but possible.

## Neighborhood Guides

Manhattan is the iconic core divided into distinct neighborhoods. Midtown is the commercial heart with Times Square, the Empire State Building, and 5th Avenue shopping — touristy but essential. The West Village and Greenwich Village are charming with brownstone streets, excellent restaurants, and a bohemian history. SoHo and Tribeca offer upscale shopping, galleries, and celebrity spotting. Chinatown and Little Italy in Lower Manhattan offer authentic food and culture. The Lower East Side is the hipster nightlife hub. Brooklyn has become as compelling as Manhattan — Williamsburg for dining and nightlife, DUMBO for stunning Manhattan views, and Park Slope for a relaxed neighborhood feel. Harlem offers rich African-American cultural history and excellent soul food.

## Transportation Tips

The NYC Subway runs 24 hours a day, 7 days a week — it is the most efficient way to get around Manhattan and the outer boroughs. Use an OMNY contactless card or your bank card directly on subway turnstiles — the old MetroCard system is being phased out. A single ride costs $2.90 with a daily cap of $34. Yellow cabs are metered and plentiful in Manhattan — tip 15-20%. Uber and Lyft are widely used. Walking is often the best option in Manhattan especially between nearby neighborhoods. The PATH train connects Manhattan to New Jersey. JFK is connected by the AirTrain to the subway. LaGuardia has no rail connection — take a bus or taxi. Avoid renting a car in Manhattan — parking is extremely expensive and traffic is brutal.

## Packing Suggestions

Comfortable walking shoes are essential — New Yorkers walk miles daily. Pack layers as temperatures can vary significantly between seasons and the contrast between outdoor heat and heavily air-conditioned interiors in summer is dramatic. A compact day bag with secure zip pockets is advisable given pickpocket risks in crowded areas like Times Square. Bring a reusable water bag — NYC tap water is among the best in the country. Smart casual clothing is appropriate for nicer restaurants and some rooftop bars have dress codes. An umbrella is useful year-round. In winter pack seriously warm layers — wind chill between buildings can be severe.

## General Travel Tips

**Currency:** The US Dollar (USD) is the currency. Cards are universally accepted. Contactless payment is widely available. ATMs are everywhere — avoid ones with high surcharge fees, typically found in convenience stores and bars.
**Language:** English is the primary language. New York is extraordinarily multilingual — Spanish, Mandarin, Russian, and dozens of other languages are widely spoken across the city.
**Safety:** New York is significantly safer than its reputation suggests. Use common sense in unfamiliar neighborhoods especially late at night. Keep bags secure in crowded areas. Emergency number is 911.
**Connectivity:** US SIM cards are available at the airport from AT&T, T-Mobile, and Verizon. T-Mobile offers good coverage throughout the city. Most hotels, cafes, and restaurants offer free WiFi.
**Tipping:** Tipping is essential in the US service industry. Restaurant servers expect 18-20%, taxi drivers 15-20%, hotel housekeeping $2-5 per night, bartenders $1-2 per drink. Failing to tip adequately is considered very rude.
