# Bangkok, Thailand — Travel Guide

## Visa and Entry Requirements

Thailand offers visa-free entry to citizens of many countries. US, UK, EU, Australian, and Canadian citizens can enter Thailand visa-free for 60 days (recently extended from 30 days) for tourism purposes. A valid passport with at least six months validity is required along with proof of onward travel. On arrival you will complete a TM6 arrival card and may be asked to show proof of funds. Thailand has also introduced a Tourist Visa (TR) for longer stays. Bangkok's main international airport is Suvarnabhumi (BKK) with Don Mueang (DMK) handling many low-cost carriers. Always check the latest entry requirements as Thailand periodically updates its visa policies and exemption lists.

## Cultural Tips and Etiquette

Thai culture is deeply influenced by Buddhism and a strong sense of respect and hierarchy. The concept of "saving face" is important — avoid confrontation or public displays of anger. The wai (pressing palms together and bowing slightly) is the traditional greeting — as a visitor you can wai back when greeted or offer a smile and nod. The monarchy is deeply revered and criticism is illegal under lèse-majesté laws — never speak disrespectfully about the royal family. Remove shoes before entering temples, homes, and many traditional businesses. Cover shoulders and knees when visiting temples — sarongs are usually available to borrow at the entrance. Never point your feet toward Buddha images or people as feet are considered the lowest and least sacred part of the body. The head is considered sacred — do not touch anyone's head.

## Seasonal Advice

Bangkok has three seasons. The Cool Season (November to February) is the best time to visit with temperatures around 25-30°C (77-86°F) and low humidity — this is peak tourist season so book accommodation in advance. The Hot Season (March to May) is extremely hot and humid with temperatures regularly hitting 38-40°C (100-104°F) — Songkran (Thai New Year water festival) in April is a spectacular cultural experience but the heat can be exhausting. The Wet Season (June to October) brings daily afternoon monsoon showers — the heat is still intense and flooding can occur in some areas. However accommodation prices drop significantly and the city is less crowded. Always carry a light rain jacket or compact umbrella in the wet season.

## Neighborhood Guides

Bangkok is a sprawling megacity but key areas are well-connected. Sukhumvit is the expat and tourist hub with excellent international dining, nightlife on Khao San Road's more grown-up cousin Soi 11, and easy BTS Skytrain access. Silom and Sathorn are the business and upscale dining districts with Patpong Night Market and Lumphini Park. Rattanakosin is the historic royal island containing the Grand Palace, Wat Pho (reclining Buddha), and Wat Arun (Temple of Dawn) — the essential cultural and historical heart of the city. Banglamphu and Khao San Road is the backpacker hub beloved by budget travelers and party-goers. Ari and Thonglor are the trendy local neighborhoods popular with young Bangkok residents offering excellent cafes, brunch spots, and boutiques away from the tourist trail.

## Transportation Tips

Bangkok traffic is legendary — always factor in significant travel time and use the elevated transport systems where possible. The BTS Skytrain (two lines) and MRT Subway are fast, air-conditioned, and inexpensive — the Rabbit Card works on BTS and is worth getting for regular use. The BTS connects Suvarnabhumi Airport via the Airport Rail Link to Phaya Thai station. Tuk-tuks are iconic but primarily for tourists — always negotiate the fare before getting in. Metered taxis are widely available and affordable — always insist on the meter (mit-er in Thai). Grab (the Southeast Asian Uber equivalent) is the most reliable option for price certainty. The Chao Phraya Express Boat is an excellent and scenic way to travel along the river connecting major temples and neighborhoods.

## Packing Suggestions

Pack lightweight, breathable clothing — Bangkok is hot and humid year-round. Long sleeve shirts and lightweight pants or maxi skirts are both comfortable and suitable for temple visits. Bring flip-flops or easy slip-on shoes for the frequent shoe removal required at temples. Pack a light cardigan or wrap for heavily air-conditioned shopping malls, restaurants, and transport — the contrast between outdoor heat and indoor AC is extreme. Sunscreen with high SPF is essential. A compact umbrella or rain poncho is essential during wet season. Pack stomach remedies as food hygiene standards vary. A power bank for your phone is very useful for navigation in a city this size.

## General Travel Tips

**Currency:** Thailand uses the Thai Baht (THB). Cash is king in Bangkok — street food, markets, tuk-tuks, and many smaller restaurants are cash-only. ATMs are widely available but charge a flat fee of around 220 THB per withdrawal — withdraw larger amounts to minimize fees. Super Rich currency exchange booths offer better rates than airport exchanges.
**Language:** Thai is the official language. English is widely spoken in tourist areas, hotels, and malls. Learning khob khun krap/ka (thank you — krap for men, ka for women) and sawadee (hello) is appreciated. Always end polite sentences with krap or ka.
**Safety:** Bangkok is generally safe for tourists. Be wary of tuk-tuk drivers offering to take you to a "special temple" or "gem store" — these are well-known tourist scams. Drink from sealed bottles only. Emergency number is 191 for police and 1669 for ambulance.
**Connectivity:** Thai SIM cards from AIS, DTAC, or TrueMove are available at the airport arrival hall and are excellent value — tourist SIM packages with generous data allowances are very affordable. Most hotels, cafes, and malls offer free WiFi.
**Tipping:** Tipping is not traditional in Thai culture but is appreciated in tourist-facing businesses. Leave 20-50 THB at restaurants for good service, tip massage therapists 50-100 THB, and round up taxi fares. Luxury hotels and upscale restaurants increasingly expect Western-style tipping.
