# Tokyo, Japan — Travel Guide

## Visa and Entry Requirements

Most visitors from the US, UK, Canada, Australia, and EU countries can enter Japan visa-free for up to 90 days for tourism purposes. You will need a valid passport with at least six months validity beyond your stay. On arrival you will be asked to provide proof of onward travel and sufficient funds for your stay. Japan does not require a tourist visa for most Western nationalities but does require a valid return or onward ticket. Citizens of some countries require a visa in advance — check the Japanese Ministry of Foreign Affairs website before traveling. Japan implemented a Visit Japan Web system for digital health and customs declarations which is recommended to complete before arrival to speed up immigration.

## Cultural Tips and Etiquette

Japan has a deeply rooted culture of respect and politeness that visitors should be mindful of. Bowing is the standard greeting — a slight nod is sufficient for tourists though locals will appreciate the effort. Removing shoes before entering homes, traditional ryokan inns, and many temples is mandatory — look for a step up at the entrance and a shoe rack as cues. Tipping is not customary in Japan and can actually be considered rude or confusing — exceptional service is simply expected. Eating and drinking while walking is frowned upon in many areas, particularly near temples and in smaller towns. Speak quietly on public transport and set your phone to silent. Queuing is taken seriously — always wait in line and board trains in an orderly fashion. When visiting temples and shrines dress modestly and follow posted instructions for purification rituals.

## Seasonal Advice

Tokyo has four distinct seasons each offering a unique experience. Spring (March to May) is the most popular time to visit with cherry blossom season peaking in late March to early April — expect crowds and higher accommodation prices, book well in advance. Summer (June to August) is hot and humid with temperatures reaching 35°C (95°F) — the rainy season in June brings daily showers. Autumn (September to November) offers stunning foliage with comfortable temperatures making it the second most popular season. Winter (December to February) is cold but dry and crowds are thinner — Christmas illuminations are spectacular and New Year celebrations are culturally significant. Golden Week (late April to early May) and Obon (mid-August) are major Japanese holidays when domestic travel surges and accommodation books up months ahead.

## Neighborhood Guides

Tokyo is a city of distinct neighborhoods each with its own character. Shinjuku is the bustling commercial heart with the famous crossing, department stores, and the lively Golden Gai bar district. Shibuya is home to the iconic scramble crossing and youth fashion culture. Harajuku offers the quirky Takeshita Street alongside the serene Meiji Shrine. Asakusa is Tokyo's most traditional district with Senso-ji Temple and old-school craft shops along Nakamise Street. Akihabara is the electronics and anime hub. Ginza is Tokyo's upscale shopping and dining district. Yanaka offers a rare glimpse of pre-war Tokyo with narrow lanes and independent shops. For a local residential feel explore Shimokitazawa with its vintage clothing stores and live music venues.

## Transportation Tips

Tokyo has one of the world's most efficient public transport systems. The Tokyo Metro and Toei Subway cover most tourist areas — a 24, 48, or 72-hour metro pass offers unlimited rides and good value. The JR Yamanote Line loops around the city connecting major districts. Get a Suica or Pasmo IC card on arrival — these rechargeable cards work on all trains, subways, buses, and even convenience stores, eliminating the need to buy individual tickets. Taxis are widely available but expensive — use them for late-night travel when trains stop running around midnight. IC Express trains connect Narita and Haneda airports to the city center. For day trips the Japan Rail Pass covers Shinkansen bullet trains to Kyoto, Osaka, and beyond.

## Packing Suggestions

Pack comfortable walking shoes as Tokyo involves significant walking. Bring or purchase a portable wifi router or SIM card for navigation — Google Maps works excellently in Tokyo. A small day bag is useful for temple visits and shopping. Carry cash as many smaller restaurants, temples, and local shops are cash-only — ATMs at 7-Eleven and Japan Post reliably accept foreign cards. Pack layers for spring and autumn when temperatures fluctuate significantly throughout the day. A compact umbrella is essential year-round. Modest clothing is recommended for temple visits.

## General Travel Tips

**Currency:** Japan is predominantly cash-based. The currency is the Japanese Yen (JPY). Withdraw cash at 7-Eleven, Lawson, or Japan Post ATMs which reliably accept foreign cards. Credit cards are increasingly accepted in larger establishments but always carry cash as backup.
**Language:** Japanese is the official language. English signage is common in tourist areas and on major train lines. Learning a few phrases — arigatou gozaimasu (thank you), sumimasen (excuse me), eigo wo hanasemasu ka (do you speak English?) — is appreciated.
**Safety:** Tokyo is one of the world's safest cities. Petty crime is extremely rare. Emergency number is 110 for police and 119 for ambulance and fire.
**Connectivity:** Pocket WiFi rentals are available at the airport and are very reliable. Local SIM cards from IIJmio or travel SIMs from the airport are also excellent options.
**Tipping:** Never tip in Japan. It is not part of the culture and may cause confusion or offense.
