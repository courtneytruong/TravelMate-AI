# Paris, France — Travel Guide

## Visa and Entry Requirements

France is part of the Schengen Area. Citizens of the US, Canada, Australia, and most Western countries can visit visa-free for up to 90 days within any 180-day period. A valid passport is required with at least three months validity beyond your intended stay. EU citizens can enter with a national ID card. From 2025 the ETIAS pre-registration system will be required for visa-exempt travelers — verify current requirements before booking. There is no arrival card required but customs declarations apply for goods above duty-free limits. Paris Charles de Gaulle and Orly airports both have efficient immigration processing for Schengen arrivals.

## Cultural Tips and Etiquette

Parisians have a reputation for being reserved but are generally warm once basic courtesies are observed. Always greet with bonjour (good morning/day) when entering a shop, restaurant, or when addressing anyone — skipping this greeting is considered rude. A light handshake is standard for formal introductions while two kisses on the cheek is common among friends. French people take meals seriously — eating on the go is frowned upon and lunch breaks are genuinely taken. Dress well — Parisians are style-conscious and dressing neatly shows respect. In restaurants do not ask for a doggy bag as it is considered poor form though this is slowly changing. Queue politely and avoid speaking loudly in public spaces. Making an effort to speak French even poorly is greatly appreciated.

## Seasonal Advice

Paris is beautiful year-round though each season has distinct characteristics. Spring (April to June) is widely considered the best time to visit with mild temperatures, blooming gardens, and the city at its most photogenic. Summer (July to August) is warm and busy — the Eiffel Tower area and Louvre can have very long queues. Many Parisians leave the city in August creating a quieter local atmosphere. Autumn (September to November) brings beautiful golden light, harvest festivals, and fashion week in September. Winter (December to February) is cold with temperatures around 5°C (41°F) but Christmas markets, ice skating rinks, and festive lights make it magical. Paris is also less crowded and more affordable in winter.

## Neighborhood Guides

Paris is organized into 20 arrondissements (districts) spiraling outward from the center. The 1st and 4th arrondissements cover the historic Île de la Cité with Notre-Dame and the Marais district with its Jewish quarter, galleries, and excellent falafel. The 5th is the Latin Quarter with bookshops, cafes, and the Sorbonne university. Saint-Germain-des-Prés (6th) is the intellectual and literary heart with famous cafes like Café de Flore. Montmartre (18th) offers the Sacré-Coeur basilica, artist studios, and a genuine village atmosphere. The 7th is home to the Eiffel Tower and Musée d'Orsay. Le Marais is one of the city's most vibrant neighborhoods with excellent restaurants, bars, and the Centre Pompidou. Canal Saint-Martin (10th) is the hip neighborhood beloved by young Parisians.

## Transportation Tips

Paris has one of Europe's finest metro systems with 16 lines covering the entire city. A carnet of 10 tickets or a Navigo Easy card offers the best value — the same tickets work on metro, RER, buses, and trams within central Paris. The RER B line connects both airports to central Paris efficiently. Walking is one of the best ways to explore Paris — many central neighborhoods are within easy walking distance of each other. Vélib' is the city's excellent bike-share scheme with stations throughout the city. Taxis and Uber are available but traffic can be heavy during peak hours. Avoid driving in Paris — parking is extremely scarce and expensive, and many central areas are pedestrianized.

## Packing Suggestions

Pack smart casual to semi-formal clothing — Parisians dress well and you will feel more comfortable fitting in. A comfortable pair of walking shoes is essential as Paris involves extensive walking on cobblestones. Bring a compact umbrella for sudden showers which are common year-round. A good day bag with secure pockets is important given pickpocket risks at tourist sites. Bring adapters for Type E French plugs (two round pins). Sunscreen is essential in summer. A light scarf or wrap is useful for visiting churches and adds a Parisian touch to any outfit.

## General Travel Tips

**Currency:** France uses the Euro (EUR). Cards are widely accepted including contactless. ATMs are plentiful — use bank ATMs to avoid high fees. Some smaller bistros and markets are cash-only.
**Language:** French is the official language. English is widely spoken in hotels, tourist areas, and many restaurants but making an effort with basic French phrases is warmly appreciated. Bonjour, merci, s'il vous plaît, and excusez-moi will get you far.
**Safety:** Paris is generally safe but pickpocketing is common at the Eiffel Tower, Louvre, and on the Metro. Keep bags in front of you and be wary of distraction scams. Emergency number is 112.
**Connectivity:** French SIM cards from Orange, SFR, or Free Mobile are available at the airport and city center shops. Most cafes and restaurants offer free WiFi — look for the sticker in the window.
**Tipping:** A service charge (service compris) is included by law in all French restaurant bills. Additional tipping is optional — rounding up or leaving a few euros for excellent service is appreciated but not expected.
