# Rome, Italy — Travel Guide

## Visa and Entry Requirements

Italy is part of the Schengen Area. Citizens of the US, Canada, Australia, and most Western countries can visit visa-free for up to 90 days within any 180-day period. A valid passport is required with at least three months validity beyond your intended stay. EU citizens can enter with a national ID card. From 2025 the ETIAS pre-registration system will be required for visa-exempt travelers — verify current requirements before booking. There is no arrival card to complete. Rome is served by two airports — Fiumicino (Leonardo da Vinci) is the main international hub and Ciampino handles low-cost carriers. Ensure you check current ETIAS requirements as the EU implementation timeline has shifted.

## Cultural Tips and Etiquette

Italians are warm, expressive, and family-oriented. Greetings are important — a handshake for formal occasions and cheek kisses (starting left) among friends and acquaintances. Dress modestly when visiting churches — shoulders and knees must be covered to enter the Vatican, Pantheon, and most churches. Carrying a scarf or shawl solves this easily. Meal times are taken seriously — lunch is typically 1-3pm and dinner after 8pm. Asking for cappuccino after lunch or dinner will mark you as a tourist — Italians drink cappuccino only in the morning. Sitting at a table costs more than standing at the bar in many cafes — look for the price list posted at the entrance. Tipping is appreciated but modest. Speak softly in museums and churches.

## Seasonal Advice

Rome is a year-round destination but seasons vary significantly. Spring (April to June) is the best time to visit with pleasant temperatures of 18-25°C (64-77°F), blooming gardens, and Easter celebrations — Holy Week brings enormous crowds around the Vatican. Summer (July to August) is hot and humid with temperatures regularly exceeding 35°C (95°F) — major attractions have long queues and the city is crowded. Many Romans leave in August making some local restaurants close. Autumn (September to November) is excellent with warm temperatures, fewer crowds, and harvest festivals. Winter (December to February) is mild at 8-15°C (46-59°F) — Christmas in Rome is atmospheric with nativity scenes and New Year celebrations at the Colosseum. Book Vatican and Colosseum tickets well in advance regardless of season.

## Neighborhood Guides

Rome rewards wandering but knowing the neighborhoods helps. The Historic Center contains the Pantheon, Piazza Navona, Campo de' Fiori, and the best gelato — stay here if budget allows. Trastevere is the charming medieval neighborhood across the Tiber with ivy-covered buildings, excellent trattorias, and vibrant nightlife. The Vatican area (Prati neighborhood) is convenient for early morning Vatican visits before crowds arrive. Testaccio is the working-class neighborhood that is the authentic home of Roman cuisine — the old slaughterhouse market and nightclubs make it popular with locals. Pigneto is the up-and-coming bohemian neighborhood away from tourists with good bars and street art. Monti is Rome's hip village-within-the-city with vintage shops and aperitivo bars.

## Transportation Tips

Rome's historic center is best explored on foot — many major sights including the Trevi Fountain, Spanish Steps, Pantheon, and Piazza Navona are within walking distance of each other. The Metro has only two main lines (A and B) but covers key sites including the Colosseum (Colosseo station) and Vatican (Ottaviano station). Bus and tram networks are extensive but can be slow in traffic. Buy tickets in advance from tabacchi (tobacco shops) — you cannot buy on board. Validate your ticket immediately when boarding or face a fine. Taxis are metered — only use official white taxis with the Roma Capitale seal. Fiumicino airport is connected by the Leonardo Express train to Termini station (€14, 32 minutes). Driving in Rome is extremely challenging and not recommended for tourists.

## Packing Suggestions

Comfortable walking shoes are essential — Rome's cobblestone streets (sampietrini) are beautiful but hard on feet. Pack a lightweight scarf or shawl for church visits. Bring breathable clothing in summer and layers for spring and autumn evenings. A refillable water bottle is very useful — Rome has hundreds of nasoni (small drinking fountains) with excellent free cold water throughout the city. Sunscreen and a hat are essential in summer. A small crossbody bag with a secure zip reduces pickpocket risk. Bring a portable phone charger for long days of sightseeing. Smart casual attire is appropriate for evening dining.

## General Travel Tips

**Currency:** Italy uses the Euro (EUR). Cards are widely accepted though some smaller trattorias, markets, and churches are cash-only. Always carry some cash. ATMs (Bancomat) are plentiful — use bank ATMs to avoid high fees.
**Language:** Italian is the official language. English is widely spoken in tourist areas, hotels, and major restaurants. Learning prego (you're welcome/please), grazie (thank you), scusi (excuse me), and dove è (where is) is helpful and appreciated.
**Safety:** Rome is generally safe but pickpocketing is a serious concern particularly at the Colosseum, Trevi Fountain, Termini station, and on crowded buses. Use a money belt or secure inner pocket. Emergency number is 112.
**Connectivity:** Italian SIM cards from TIM, Vodafone, or WindTre are available at the airport and throughout the city. Most restaurants and cafes offer free WiFi — ask for the password (password WiFi).
**Tipping:** Tipping is not mandatory in Italy. A coperto (cover charge) of €1-3 per person is standard at sit-down restaurants and is not a tip. Rounding up or leaving €1-2 for good service is appreciated. Do not feel obligated to tip large amounts as in the US.
